# SharkTrack Users and Collaborators Research Report

*Comprehensive analysis of institutions, researchers, and BRUV studies
using or potentially using SharkTrack software (2020-2024)*

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Executive Summary

This report identifies and analyses the research teams and institutions
currently using SharkTrack for BRUV (Baited Remote Underwater Video)
analysis, documenting their published research, methodologies, and
potential for species classification model development. These
collaborators represent a global network of marine biologists working
across diverse ecosystems, from tropical coral reefs to temperate
continental shelves.

**Key Finding**: These research groups collectively possess extensive
BRUV datasets spanning multiple species, habitats, and geographic
regions - making them ideal partners for collaborative species
classification model development.

## Detailed Research Profiles

### üá¨üáß University of Exeter Team

**Principal Investigators**: Dr. Francesco Garzon, Dr. Matthew J. Witt,
Adam Whiting **Research Focus**: Nurse shark ecology, marine protected
area effectiveness

#### Key Publications (2020-2024):

1.  **"Nation-wide assessment of the distribution and population size of
    the data-deficient nurse shark (Ginglymostoma cirratum)"** (2021)
    -   *Authors*: Francesco Garzon, Rachel T Graham, Ivy Baremore, et
        al.
    -   *Published*: PLoS One
    -   *Methodology*: Combination of transect surveys and baited remote
        underwater videos (BRUVs)
    -   *Key Finding*: First national assessment giving total nurse
        shark population estimate of 3,858 to 14,375 sharks in Belize
2.  **"SharkTrack: an accurate, generalisable software for streamlining
    shark and ray underwater video analysis"** (2024)
    -   *Authors*: Filippo Varini, Joel H. Gayford, Jeremy Jenrette,
        Matthew J. Witt, Francesco Garzon, et al.
    -   *Published*: arXiv preprint
    -   *Impact*: 95% reduction in manual analysis time, 89% accuracy
        over 207 hours of footage
3.  **"Application of spatially robust stereo‚ÄêBRUV sampling for
    quantifying fish assemblages in UK marine protected areas"** (2025)
    -   *Lead Author*: Owen M. Exeter
    -   *Published*: Ecological Applications
    -   *Methodology*: Comprehensive stereo-BRUV surveys in UK MPAs

#### Metadata Characteristics:

-   **Temperature Range**: 18-28¬∞C (temperate to tropical)
-   **Depth Range**: 5-40m (coastal to shelf)
-   **Substrate Types**: Coral reef, sandy lagoons, rocky outcrops

## Recommendations for Collaboration

### 1. **Species Classification Model Exchange**

-   **Primary Target**: Request `classifier.pt` and `class_mapping.txt`
    files from each research group
-   **Benefit**: Rapid deployment of region-specific models across
    global research network
-   **Implementation**: Standardised model format for SharkTrack
    integration

### 2. **Collaborative Training Database**

-   **Approach**: Pool annotated video datasets from all research groups
-   **Outcome**: Development of universal elasmobranch classifier
-   **Technical**: Implement metadata standardisation system developed
    for this project

### 3. **Cross-Regional Validation**

-   **Method**: Test regional models on other geographic datasets
-   **Purpose**: Assess model transferability and identify regional
    biases
-   **Research Output**: Publications on geographic variation in species
    classification accuracy

### 4. **Metadata Standardisation**

-   **Implementation**: Deploy auto-population system to all
    collaborating institutions
-   **Benefit**: Consistent data collection across global network
-   **Timeline**: Could be implemented alongside model sharing
    initiative

## Conclusion

The research groups identified represent a comprehensive global network
of BRUV-based elasmobranch research spanning tropical to temperate
ecosystems. Their collective expertise in species-specific research,
combined with the enhanced SharkTrack platform developed in this
project, creates an unprecedented opportunity for collaborative marine
biodiversity research.

The technical infrastructure is now in place to support: - **Automated
species classification** across multiple biogeographic regions -
**Standardised metadata collection** for global research collaboration -
**Self-improving models** that learn from every deployment worldwide -
**Reduced barriers to entry** for new research groups through
auto-population systems

This collaboration could establish the first truly global, AI-enhanced
platform for elasmobranch research, dramatically accelerating marine
conservation efforts worldwide.
